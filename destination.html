Harar

Enclosed within 16th-century stone walls, Harar is a labyrinth of 82 mosques and over 100 shrines. Known as the fourth holiest city in Islam, its narrow, neon-painted alleys are alive with the scent of roasting coffee and the bright colors of traditional Harari basketry. It is a place where time slows down, and ancient traditions are whispered through every doorway

Lalibela Area

Descend into a world carved from solid volcanic rock. In the 12th century, King Lalibela sought to recreate the Holy City on Ethiopian soil, resulting in eleven monolithic churches connected by a labyrinth of tunnels and trenches. These are not built stones—they are living sculptures, still echoing with the chants of white-robed pilgrims today.

Simien Mountains

They are the crown jewel of Ethiopia's natural wonders. Often called the Roof of Africa, this UNESCO World Heritage site is a jagged landscape of massive plateaus, deep precipices, and volcanic plugs that stretch as far as the eye can see--offering world-class hikes and breathtaking views of Ethiopian highlands.

Gheralta, Tigray

Known as the "Petra of Africa," this region is home to over 120 ancient churches hidden in high-altitude caves. It is a place for the brave and the spiritual, where trekking to the sky-high Abuna Yemata Guh rewards you with breathtaking views and 5th-century frescoes.

Aksum

One of Africa's oldest continuously inhabited sites, this was the capital of the Aksumite Kingdom dating back to 100AD. Today, it is known mainly for its stellae-enormous stone obelisks up to 33m tall and weighing up to 520 tonnes-the oldest of which date back 1,700 years.

Danakil Depression

Visiting Danakil is a true expedition; you can trek up an active volcano, camp on the crater rim and explore the otherworldly landscapes of Dallol, with its geysers and rainbow coloured mineral deposits.
